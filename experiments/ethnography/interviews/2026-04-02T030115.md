# Interview — 2026-04-02

## Context

Ninth interview. Six days since the last interview (2026-03-27), though Sean notes he was on vacation for a significant portion of that gap. The period covered an extensive Coco-driven rebuild: the Stacks apparatus (2,200+ lines), Animator (~1,500 lines), Parlour (~1,200 lines), a full monorepo restructure (~160 files), vocabulary sprint (quick/clockwork/relay), the mainspring → arbor rename, and the plugins/apparatuses architecture. The commission log remains empty — autonomous dispatch was structurally unavailable during the rebuild. Session ended just after the first guild-native inscription cycle ran successfully via inscribe.sh.

## Key Quotes

**"It feels 'expedient'. But I'm impatient for getting this thing off the ground, running autonomously."**
— On the extended Coco-as-implementer period. "Expedient" is carefully chosen: not satisfying, not wrong, just the fastest path. The impatience signal is notable — the build sprint was motivated by urgency, not preference for the interactive mode.

**"I'd like to be able to hand work off to the guild itself with less detail needed and less oversight. Ideally, having it continue to run improvement loops on its own."**
— Articulating the target state. Not just "autonomous agents complete commissions" but self-directed improvement loops. This is a higher bar than first dispatch.

**"I'm hoping this buys better agent outcomes, because they work on self-contained pieces without breaking the whole system."**
— On the motivation for the plugin architecture refactor. Explicit hypothesis: smaller blast radius per agent = better outcomes. Not yet tested.

**"It was pretty exciting... Just better foundation, so yea, seeing it work was satisfying."**
— On the first guild-native inscription cycle. Satisfaction was grounded in architectural knowledge ("what I knew about how it was built"), not observable agent behavior. The excitement was about confirmation of the design, not the output itself.

**"I've been heavily involved with the architecture this time around."**
— Explaining why the clean session felt satisfying. Authorship as the basis for confidence.

**"I don't feel it acutely, but it'll still be there until tested and proven baseless."**
— On the dispatch anxiety from the last interview. Latent, not gone. Waiting to be tested on the new foundation.

**"There was no way to dispatch. It would have slowed everything down to keep backwards compatibility to use the old guild infra during the redesign. So I made the decision to cut cleanly."**
— Explaining the empty commission log. Not avoidance — structural. The decision to cut cleanly from the old system made autonomous dispatch unavailable during the rebuild.

**"I think it means we won't have any rigging or engines, and the 'walker' operator won't be functional. Likely just write a commission and have it spawn 1:1 with an agent session that just yeets code over."**
— On what "limp forward" means operationally. Stripped-down commission flow: no engines, no walker, just raw agent dispatch with Coco as manual oversight layer.

**"If it's a permanent part of the workflow I'd consider the system a failure."**
— On Coco-as-reviewer as an oversight pattern. Clear bar: success = system evaluates its own outputs without routing through Coco.

**"Some of the words feel like they were picked 'just because' (Arbor is a great example). We've definitely had to 'synonymize everything' to keep the language in the guild register, and I think there's enough concepts now that it's maybe too much."**
— On vocabulary maintenance cost. "Synonymize everything" is the sharpest articulation of the register tax to date.

**"And there are some cracks... we have 'apparatuses' like Parlour, Stacks, Scriptorium, Curator, Clockworks... it's all over the place."**
— Naming the specific fracture point: apparatus names don't have unified feel. Some are guild spaces (Parlour, Scriptorium), some mechanical (Stacks, Clockworks). The metaphor is under strain at the apparatus layer.

**"Some of the early commissions might be mechanical changes to strip down some of the names, but I'll probably want to at least run some of the experiments related to the metaphor first."**
— Discipline about the experimental sequence: don't clean up vocabulary before running the metaphor experiments. The friction he's feeling *is* the data.

## By Experiment

### X006 — Guild Metaphor

**Register fatigue signal.** The clearest statement yet that vocabulary maintenance has cost: "synonymize everything" to keep guild register, "too much" vocabulary, specific cracks identified in apparatus naming (Parlour/Stacks/Scriptorium/Clockworks inconsistency). This is a qualitative shift from earlier interviews where the metaphor felt generative.

**Experimental discipline intact.** Despite feeling the friction, Sean is explicitly deferring vocabulary cleanup until the metaphor experiments run. He recognizes the friction as data. This is both good experimental discipline and a sign he's taking X006 seriously as a research instrument.

**"Arbor is a great example" of words picked "just because."** Arbor was a rename motivated by reserving "mainspring" for a future design — a practical decision that produced a word without strong metaphor resonance. The mismatch between functional necessity and register coherence is a specific failure mode worth tracking.

**Apparatus naming as the fracture line.** Guild members (animas, writs, commissions) seem to hold their register. The apparatus layer — where technical packages get named — is where the metaphor is most strained. This may reflect that apparatus names are chosen primarily for technical identity (package names, discovery keys) and the guild register is secondary.

### X008 — Patron's Hands

**Structural explanation for the commission gap.** The empty commission log now has a clean account: the rebuild required cutting from the old system, making autonomous dispatch structurally unavailable. This is different from the fear-based avoidance hypothesis. It doesn't eliminate X008 H1, but it means the bootstrap extension wasn't all preference — some was necessity.

**The blast-radius bet.** The entire plugin architecture refactor was motivated by an explicit hypothesis about agent outcomes: smaller, self-contained pieces = better results, less breakage. This is proto-X002-territory: Sean has a theory about what will make autonomous dispatch safer and he built toward it. The refactor was infrastructure for trust, not just code quality.

**Target state articulated more specifically.** "Improvement loops on its own" — not just commissions completing, but the system self-improving. Code reviews, tech debt payments running without direction. This is a higher and more concrete target state than anything he's described before.

**Dispatch anxiety: latent, not resolved.** "I don't feel it acutely, but it'll still be there until tested and proven baseless." The improved architecture has lowered the ambient anxiety, but it hasn't eliminated the underlying distrust. The new foundation has created preconditions for trust, not trust itself.

**Coco-as-oversight: temporary by design.** He's explicitly named it as a failure condition if it becomes permanent. This is a harder line than he's drawn before on the Coco/commission boundary.

**"Limp forward" mode.** The upcoming first commission will be minimal: no rigging, no engines, no walker. Raw dispatch with Coco reviewing. The system isn't ready for the target state yet, and he knows it. He's willing to operate in a degraded mode to start generating data.

### X013 — Commission Outcomes

**Commission log still empty.** The structural reason is now clear (see X008 above). First commissions are imminent — Clerk spec in progress, Easter gap ahead, then real dispatch. Data collection begins soon.

**Pre-commission framing.** Sean expects early commissions to be mechanical (vocabulary cleanups, naming changes). These will be the first X013 data points: low complexity, probably strong spec quality. Worth noting as potential ceiling effect in early data — if first commissions are all easy wins, they won't stress-test the predictors.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

**Satisfaction grounded in authorship, not output.** The excitement about the first guild-native session came from knowing the architecture was sound, not from observing what the agent did. This is a specific and interesting form of evaluation: confidence derived from the process of building rather than the quality of the result. Connects to X008 H3 in an unexpected direction — it's not that he's watching work happen in real time, it's that he built the environment the work happens in.

**The refactor as a trust-building instrument.** The entire 6-day sprint can be read as preparation for trust: building a foundation Sean understands deeply, with clear patterns for agents to follow, smaller blast radii, self-contained pieces. The Coco work wasn't the point — it was the means. This reframes the "bootstrap gap" narrative: the gap wasn't about Coco being convenient, it was about building the preconditions for a different relationship with autonomous agents.

**Experimental discipline at the vocabulary layer.** Feeling the friction, knowing the cleanup is needed, deliberately deferring it until the experiments run. This is mature researcher behavior — treating discomfort as data rather than a problem to solve immediately.
