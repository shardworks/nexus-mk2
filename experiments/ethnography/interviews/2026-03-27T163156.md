# Interview — 2026-03-27

## Context

Eighth interview. Covers the two days since the last interview (2026-03-25), which included the most active build period yet: writs system, commission removal, conversation API, clockworks daemon, upgrade system, and the first several autonomous commissions running against the framework itself. X007 was closed as complete. The system moved from "preparing to operate" into actual operations.

## Key Quotes

**"Anything that is core/critical enough that it could break the framework I still do manually with Coco. Everything else, I try to commission. Especially if it's new or self-contained."**
— Articulating the dispatch heuristic that has emerged. First clear statement of a decision rule that he's using in practice.

**"It's intuitive.. fear-based, maybe. I get an anxiety signal if something feels dangerous to dispatch blindly."**
— On how he makes the Coco/commission call in the moment. Unprompted self-diagnosis: the heuristic isn't rational analysis, it's a somatic signal. Notable because it connects the dispatch decision directly to distrust.

**"I don't want to get blocked because of an agent doing something that breaks things. Which they still do."**
— On what the anxiety is actually about. It's about continuity — not wanting the system he now relies on to become the thing that stops him. "Which they still do" confirms the anxiety is grounded in current experience, not prior history.

**"Even while working collaboratively with Coco I was only reviewing a fraction of the code."**
— Spontaneous acknowledgment that the Coco channel doesn't actually give him code-level visibility. The perceived safety differential may be smaller than the anxiety suggests.

**"I usually talk through the plan at a pretty detailed level. And Coco flags questions that might be more impactful or have non-obvious answers. But I'm generally still trusting the right questions are being surfaced, and the implementation matches the plan.. and the plan matched my intent."**
— On what Coco actually provides. It's a chain of trust, not verification. Value is in the dialogue as plan-validation, not code review.

**"That one was documented so thoroughly because the 'first dispatch' was part of an experiment (X007 I think?) which required that rigor. The rest have just been 'building more stuff'."**
— On the difference between the careful first commission and the more rapid subsequent dispatches. The rigor was experimental, not operational. Entirely unsentimental about the shift.

**"Definitely use it. Still assessing if it's better than the more obvious 'jobs'. Not like how often I typo 'writ' into 'write', though."**
— On whether "writs" has landed as vocabulary. Adopted but still being evaluated against the simpler alternative. The typo friction is real and concrete.

**"Frustrating. Everytime something like that happens I lose a bit of confidence in the system, which only increases the anxiety around use."**
— On discovering Ergon's wrong-branch commit. The failure wasn't isolated — it compounded the general confidence problem.

**"The interesting thing about that is it's sort of a relic of building things myself.. Losing code I write is incredibly frustrating, and a big setback. But even if a whole commission is lost it doesn't really matter. There's maybe a token cost or whatever, but the whole thing can just be redone. That's an interesting thing to note, and something I'll need to internalize more."**
— Unprompted mid-interview insight. Realized in real time that his anxiety about lost work is calibrated to *his* effort, not to agent effort. A commission being lost has a token cost, not an effort cost. He named it as something he needs to internalize — not yet fully integrated.

## By Experiment

### X006 — Guild Metaphor

- **"Writs" is in use but still being assessed.** He uses the word, but is still weighing it against "jobs" — the simpler, more obvious alternative. The metaphor-fit question isn't settled. Notable that "jobs" has no guild-metaphor flavor; "writs" does. If he keeps reaching back toward "jobs," it may signal the metaphor word isn't earning its keep in daily use.
- **The typo friction is real.** "Writ" → "write" is a consistent muscle-memory error. Small but worth tracking — friction in a term's use is one indicator of adoption vs. just compliance.

### X008 — Patron's Hands

- **A stable dispatch heuristic has emerged.** The decision rule is now explicit: core/critical framework risk → Coco; new, self-contained, non-critical → commission. This is a meaningful operational development. The heuristic is *intuitive* (his word), not deliberated — it operates below the level of conscious choice.
- **The heuristic is fear-based, not capability-based.** He didn't say "Coco is better at X, agents are better at Y." He said he gets an anxiety signal. The dispatch decision is driven by distrust management, not task-type analysis. This is an important nuance for H2 (granularity split is self-organizing): the split is self-organizing, but the organizing principle is emotional/somatic, not rational.
- **The Coco channel is also a trust chain.** The perceived safety of Coco work is not code review — it's plan validation through dialogue. He trusts Coco to surface the right questions; trusts that implementation matches the plan; trusts the plan matched his intent. Each is a trust step, not a verification step. The actual visibility gap between Coco and autonomous dispatch may be smaller than the anxiety gradient suggests.
- **Complexity correlates with failure rate, but root cause is unknown.** He's observed that core lifecycle/dispatch changes produce more bugs than UI work. He cannot attribute it: instructions, commission phrasing, or model deficiency are all open. He's operating on pattern recognition, not diagnosis.
- **The "lost work" anxiety is a holdover from self-built code.** The real-time insight at the end of the interview: he caught himself feeling anxiety about Ergon's wrong-branch commit as if *his* work had been lost. He named it as "a relic of building things myself." A commission being redone costs tokens, not effort. He said explicitly he needs to internalize this — suggesting it's intellectually clear but not yet emotionally integrated. This may be one of the more significant mental model shifts to track over time.
- **Confidence erodes with each failure.** "Every time something like that happens I lose a bit of confidence in the system." The failures are cumulative in their emotional impact, even when technically recoverable. This has implications for the "body of successful work" he's hoping to build — he's tracking a ratio, not just incidents.
- **H3 (seeing changes judgment):** First real data point in the interview. He reviewed Ergon's wrong-branch commit and felt *frustrated*, not *forgiving*. The equanimity framing ("it can just be redone") emerged in retrospect, not in the moment. The in-the-moment reaction was standard loss/frustration.

### X007 — First Contact

- **Closed and not mourned.** He identified X007 by name when prompted, as "the first dispatch" that required rigor. No residual attachment to the careful first-dispatch mode. The experiment ending coincided exactly with operations beginning. That transition was clean and deliberate.

## Cross-Cutting Observations

The most significant finding from this interview is the *cost-of-effort asymmetry* he articulated at the end. His anxiety about agent failures is partly miscalibrated — calibrated to the cost of *his* lost effort (enormous), when agent work costs tokens (trivial). He named this himself, called it a relic, and said he needs to internalize it. If that internalization happens, it could meaningfully lower his anxiety floor and change how permissive he is with autonomous dispatch. Worth watching over the next several interviews whether this insight shifts his behavior or stays theoretical.

The trust chain through Coco is worth flagging as a structural observation: the interactive agent doesn't actually give him code-level verification — it gives him conversational confidence. The question "are the right questions being surfaced?" is itself a trust judgment he can't verify from outside. In that sense, the Coco channel and the commission channel may be more similar than they feel.
