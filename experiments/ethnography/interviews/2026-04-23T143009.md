## By Experiment

### X006 — Guild Metaphor

**Individual anima personas are quietly retired.** Sean explicitly said the concept
"wasn't adding anything" and has been "back-burnered." The guild metaphor works at the
role level (sage, artificer, primer), but individual named animas running 50 concurrent
commissions breaks the suspension of disbelief. Roles have become the operational model;
individual naming is an acknowledged "kludgey" edge.

This is a significant H1 finding. The *role* layer of the metaphor is sticky and
functional. The *individual identity* layer is not. Sean doesn't think of agents as
"John the Builder" with a lineage — he thinks of them as roles instantiated on demand.
The guild vocabulary operates below conscious attention (he "just knew" analyst was too
modern) but doesn't extend to individual agent personas.

**Sage-primer naming as evidence for background vocabulary adoption.** When naming the
new role, Sean didn't deliberate in guild-register terms — he felt the wrongness of
"analyst" immediately. The vocabulary operates as tacit aesthetic judgment, not as a
framework he applies consciously. That's a strong H1 signal: the metaphor has been
internalized, not just referenced.

### X008 — Patron's Hands

**H6 (Mountain-to-Spec) is not the target state — it's a waypoint.** Sean articulated
this explicitly: he wants to move beyond "pointing at mountains." The target is a system
that "knows what I want" and self-generates direction. The patron-anima/taste-capture work
is the first concrete step toward this extended target, but Sean is skeptical it will
fully work.

**Taste is harder to codify than expected.** Two specific concerns surfaced:
(1) Sean believes his thinking has more complexity than can be captured in a few dozen
principles; (2) The principles list was authored by Coco through mining, not by Sean
directly, and he knows some aren't quite right. He accepted the MVP anyway — but with
eyes open about the fidelity gap.

**Stage 1 is substantially in place; Stage 3 is the real blocker.** When asked about
the nearest gap to unattended operation, Sean skipped Stage 1 and named Stage 3
(self-commissioning from vision) as the key missing piece. Stage 1 infrastructure (retry
apparatus, rig machinery) is mostly functional — the SQLite surgery this morning was an
edge case that the newly-installed retry plugin would have handled (at higher cost).

**The "magic" framing.** Sean explicitly called his vision "magic," and contrasted it
with Coco's "much more realistic & sensible system." This is a new data point: the
patron's target state may be aspirationally disconnected from near-term system
capability. "Lack of creativity and vision" in the system is named as a core challenge —
not a bug to fix, but a fundamental property of current agent behavior that the
self-commissioning work is meant to overcome.

### X013 — Commission Outcomes

## Cross-Cutting Observations

**The patron's target state has evolved beyond X008's hypotheses.** X008 H6 described
the target as "pointing at mountains" — patron provides direction, system generates spec.
Sean has now articulated a target beyond that: a system that generates its own direction
from internalized vision, without patron input at the mountain level. This is a
significant extension. The hypotheses may need updating to reflect this.

**The verification gap as the most important current blocker.** Across commission
probing, autonomy stage analysis, and the "magic" conversation, the same gap recurs: the
system cannot yet reliably verify that its own output is correct. Unit tests and static
analysis catch structural errors; they don't catch behavioral regressions like "every
session gets zombie-reaped after 5 minutes." Until the system can self-verify, genuine
autonomy is constrained.

**Sean operates from an increasingly high-altitude mental model.** "I don't even really
know what's in the codebase." This is the natural endpoint of the trust-and-delegate
pattern — but it means complexity estimates are calibrated against the conceptual model,
not implementation reality. This is partly by design (thin specs, mountain-level direction)
and partly a consequence of the pace and volume of autonomous work. The gap between
mental model and actual codebase will compound over time.

### Observations

**The experiment infrastructure has a human-motivation problem.** The commission log,
manual complexity annotations, ethnography scheduling — these require consistent patron
discipline. Under MVP time pressure, that discipline collapsed. The data gaps are real:
brief authorship data is contaminated (Coco-generated, patron semi-reviewing), outcome
and retry metadata is missing for recent commissions, the ethnography gap is 16 days.

**The contamination is structural, not behavioral.** Sean's description of why he can't
annotate honestly is precise: Coco is drafting the briefs, he's only semi-reviewing them.
The "patron self-assessment at dispatch time" that X013 H1/H2 depends on is not patron
self-assessment when the patron didn't write the spec. This isn't a discipline problem;
it's a workflow mismatch. The experiment was designed for a patron who wrote specs; the
system has evolved so that Coco drafts them.

**The minimum viable research goal reframes the data question.** Sean doesn't need
comprehensive X013 data to write about the project. He needs evidence for one
non-intuitive finding. That's a much smaller target than the full experimental apparatus.
The question of what that finding is — and whether the existing data is sufficient to
support it — is worth a dedicated session.

**"Automatic/transparent" as the design constraint.** Sean's diagnosis is clear: data
capture that requires manual friction won't happen under time pressure. Whatever research
infrastructure continues needs to be integrated into the existing workflow (commission
dispatch, session wrapup, planner outputs) rather than layered on top of it.
