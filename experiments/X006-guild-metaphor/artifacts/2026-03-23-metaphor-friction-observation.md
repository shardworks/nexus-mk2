# X006 Observation — First Metaphor-Reality Friction

**Date:** 2026-03-23
**Source:** Coco session — vocabulary refinement (session 3 of the day)
**Observer:** Coco (not an ethnographer interview — this is an in-session observation)

## The Moment

While renaming the "summon engine" (the mechanical process that assembles an anima's identity and launches a Claude session), Sean challenged the name by asking what the engine *actually does* and how it looks in the guildhall metaphor. This produced the first clear instance where the metaphor's fiction collided with implementation reality.

## The Tension

**The metaphor implies animas are persistent.** They're guild members. They're in the register, on the roster. They have names, histories, compositions. The fiction is that they're *always there* — hanging around the guildhall, available to be called on. A standing anima is "always there, always available."

**The reality is that animas are reconstituted from scratch every session.** Each time the guild needs an anima, a brand new Claude session is created. The anima has no memory of previous sessions. The engine must assemble their complete identity — codex, curriculum, temperament, implement instructions — and deliver it fresh. The anima doesn't "walk to the workbench." The anima is *built* at the workbench.

**The compose engine is where this seam is most visible.** Every other part of the system can maintain the fiction: the register "remembers" animas, the roster shows "who's available," commissions are "assigned to" existing members. But the compose engine does the thing that the metaphor says shouldn't need to happen — it creates the anima's lived experience of identity from institutional records, every single time.

## How It Surfaced

The naming progression tells the story:

1. **"Summon"** — original name. Implies calling an entity from somewhere else. Sean flagged: "this is basically them receiving a letter or a page giving them a message, isn't it?" He was trying to keep the fiction: the anima exists, the engine just contacts them.

2. **"Marshal"** — proposed replacement. Implies assembling resources and preparing for action. Closer to reality but still carries "sending someone to work" energy — assumes the someone already exists.

3. **Sean's pivotal observation:** "This was very focused on the 'delivering commissions' aspect. But part of this — assembling the agent instructions for an anima, essentially — might have other uses: creating an interactive session for me to consult with an anima, or even facilitating conversations between two different animas. The 'put tools on a table' is a fictional illusion since everything is just putting the knowledge of the tools into the agent's head."

4. **"Compose"** — final name. Chosen because it honestly describes what happens (assembling identity from components) without pretending the anima was already there. The engine *composes* the anima — like composing music from notes, or composing a letter from thoughts. The components exist in the guild's records; the compose engine makes them a living whole.

## Why This Matters for X006

This is direct evidence for the experiment's core questions:

- **H1 (Human Connection):** Sean is deeply engaged with getting the metaphor *right* — not just decorative, but accurate to how the system actually works. When the metaphor stretched past what he believed ("summon" implying the anima was always there), he pushed back. He didn't accept a comfortable fiction; he wanted a name that honestly described the mechanism. This suggests the metaphor's value to Sean is *legibility*, not *atmosphere*.

- **Metaphor collapse risk (documented in spec):** "Some concepts may not survive contact with implementation. If a concept doesn't map cleanly to how the system actually works, forcing the metaphor will create confusion rather than clarity." This is exactly that moment — and notably, Sean resolved it by adjusting the vocabulary to match reality rather than forcing reality into the fiction. The metaphor bent; it didn't break.

- **The persistence illusion is load-bearing.** The entire guild metaphor depends on animas being persistent identities. But they aren't — not in the way humans are persistent. The compose engine is the mechanism that *creates* the illusion of persistence. This is the most architecturally honest part of the system: it's the seam where "guild member with a name and history" meets "stateless AI session that lasts 30 minutes." The decision to call it "compose" rather than "summon" or "marshal" is a decision to acknowledge the seam rather than paper over it.

## Sean's Key Quotes

- "The 'put tools on a table' is a fictional illusion since everything is just putting the knowledge of the tools into the agent's head. Does this affect the name?"
- "I don't think we need a metaphor concept, probably just can live in architecture desc — implementation detail I'd say."
- "Any way to describe what this engine is doing and how it looks in the metaphor without stretching it too much?"

The last quote is the interesting one: he wants to keep the metaphor coherent but is explicitly aware of the stretch. He's asking for honest fiction, not comfortable fiction.
